Section 1 - The Spectrum
```

What Is Light?

Light is a form of electromagnetic (EM) radiation that travels as waves. The distance between wave peaks is called the wavelength, measured in nanometers (nm).

Visible light is only a tiny slice of the full EM spectrum. Below are the wavelengths we can see:

380 nm440500560620680700 nm

Different wavelengths appear as different colors. Violet has the shortest visible wavelength; Red has the longest.

Continuous Spectrum

When white light (like sunlight) passes through a prism, it spreads into a continuous spectrum - a smooth rainbow of all visible colors blending together with no gaps.

This happens because the sun emits many different wavelengths of light at once.

Absorption Spectra

When white light passes through a gas, atoms in the gas absorb specific wavelengths of light. This creates an absorption spectrum - a continuous rainbow with dark lines where specific wavelengths were absorbed.

Think of it like this: each element has a unique set of "favorite" wavelengths it likes to absorb. The dark lines are like a fingerprint of that element.

Example: Hydrogen absorption - notice the dark gaps

Emission Spectra

When a gas is heated or energized, its electrons jump to higher energy levels, then fall back down - releasing energy as light. This creates an emission spectrum - bright colored lines on a black background.

The bright lines in an emission spectrum are at the exact same wavelengths as the dark lines in that element's absorption spectrum. Same fingerprint, just inverted!

Example: Hydrogen emission - bright lines only

Why Are Spectra Unique?

Every element has electrons arranged at specific energy levels. When electrons move between levels, they absorb or emit only the exact amount of energy matching that transition. Since each element has a unique electron arrangement, each element produces a unique spectral fingerprint.

Scientists use this to identify elements in distant stars, galaxies, and even the atmospheres of other planets - all from the light that reaches us!

Section 3 - Star ID
```

Reading Star Spectra

Astronomers aim telescopes at stars and pass their light through a spectrograph. The resulting spectrum shows absorption lines - dark gaps where elements in the star's outer atmosphere absorbed specific wavelengths.

By matching these dark lines to known element fingerprints, we can identify exactly which elements are present - even in stars billions of light-years away!

Doppler Shift of Spectra

Just like how an ambulance siren sounds higher-pitched when approaching and lower when moving away, light waves also shift based on motion.

Blue Shift: When a star moves toward us, its light waves are compressed - spectral lines shift toward shorter wavelengths (bluer).

Red Shift: When a star moves away from us, its light waves are stretched - spectral lines shift toward longer wavelengths (redder).

The further the spectral lines shift from their normal position, the faster the star is moving.
